We've composed this book to help you become a stronger communicator. Toward that end, in this book we take a rhetorical approach—as we explain in Part 1, which you are just now starting.

We have also designed this book around learning outcomes from the Council of Writing Program Administrators (WPA), a group focused on improving writing instruction in the United States.

Below are the learning outcomes for which Part 1 prepares you. (There are more outcomes you will encounter in later parts.) If you do not know the terms, Part 1 will deﬁne them for you—and the rest of the book will help you develop this very important knowledge.

Focus on a purpose: As a composer of texts, do you understand how to articulate a purpose for your text and then to start shaping your text around that purpose? Do you also understand that your purpose might shift was you develop your text?

Respond to the needs of different audiences: As a composer of texts, do you understand how to describe those for whom you compose and then how to shape texts for them?

Respond appropriately to different kinds of rhetorical situations: As a composer of texts, can you describe the contexts in which your audience(s) receive the texts you compose for them, and how those contexts shape audiences' responses?

Understand a writing assignment as a series of tasks, including ﬁnding, evaluating, analyzing, and synthesizing appropriate primary and secondary sources: By being able to differentiate the tasks that go into composing a text, are you able to help yourself understand what you need to do at any point in your composing process?
