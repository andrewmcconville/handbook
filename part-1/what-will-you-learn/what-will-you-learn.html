We’ve composed this book to help you become a stronger communicator. Toward that end, in this book we take a rhetorical approach—as we explain in Part 1, which you are just now starting.

We have also designed this book around learning outcomes from the Council of Writing Program Administrators (WPA), a group focused on improving writing instruction in the United States.

Below are the learning outcomes for which Part 1 prepares you. (There are more outcomes you will encounter in later parts.) If you do not know the terms, Part 1 will deﬁne them for you—and the rest of the book will help you develop this very important knowledge.

The WPA Outcomes say that “Writers develop rhetorical knowledge by negotiating purpose, audience, context, and conventions as they compose a variety of texts for different situations.” In Part 1, you learn about:

Audiences: As a composer of texts, do you understand how to describe those for whom you compose and then how to shape texts for them?

Purpose: As a composer of texts, do you understand how to articulate a purpose for your text and then to start shaping your text around that purpose? Do you also understand that your purpose might shift was you develop your text?

Different Situations: As a composer of texts, can you describe the contexts in which your audience(s) receive the texts you compose for them, and how those contexts shape audiences’ responses?

The WPA Outcomes say that “Writers use multiple strategies, or composing processes, to conceptualize, develop, and finalize projects. Composing processes are seldom linear: a writer may research a topic before drafting, then conduct additional research while revising or after consulting a colleague.”

In Part 1, you are introduced to the steps of one PROCESS for composing a project that requires research. You will see this process at work if you move through this handbook in order; you can also see this process at work in one writer’s development of a research paper.
