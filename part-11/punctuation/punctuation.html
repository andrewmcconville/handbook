Punctuation shows readers the shape of your ideas, where you want emphasis, and when you use others’ words.

Punctuation helps readers read.

Before writing, punctuation did not exist. People simply spoke, not thinking of what they said as being words much less as being divided by punctuation. People simply put sounds together in different orders—and they understood each other.

After the invention of writing, many centuries passed before people invented punctuation. (Even spaces between words had to be invented.)

Historically, punctuation appeared when speakers needed to read printed words aloud to listeners, as in churches. Imagine reading this passage aloud, and you should understan why punctuation exists:

most punctuation marks are composed to be seen but not heard these subtle often understated devices are quite important however for they are the meter that determines the measure within the silent voice of typography punctuation directs tempo pitch volume and the separation of words periods signify full stops commas slow the reader down question marks change pitch quotation marks indicate references

The information in this part helps you make informed decisions about how to punctuate your writing to reach your specific audiences.
